How to use the template

The template is plug-and-play for the request language, but the legal citations and the custodian (who you address it to) are state-specific. Here is the minimum you need to change before sending it to a New Mexico agency:

  1. Swap the legal authority. Replace every reference to Indiana's Access to Public Records Act with New Mexico's Inspection of Public Records Act, NMSA 1978 §§ 14-2-1 et seq. The state AG publishes a plain-language IPRA guide that explains the timelines (15 days for non-trust agencies, 3 days for law-enforcement records under certain exceptions).
  2. Address it to the right custodian. For the Alamogordo Police Department and the City of Alamogordo, the records custodian is typically the City Clerk. The current contact is on the city website. Law-enforcement records may need to go through the Department and the City Attorney.
  3. Keep the substance. The template's list of document categories (contracts, MOUs, procurement, audit logs, hot lists, data-sharing partners, etc.) is the part that actually works — it forces the agency to either produce or formally deny each one. Don't trim it.
  4. Ask for a fee waiver. New Mexico law allows fee waivers when the request serves the public interest. The Business Reform template doesn't include one; add a short paragraph citing NMSA 1978 § 14-2-9 and the public-interest factors.
  5. Save your copy. If they ignore you, miss the deadline, or release only part, the next step is the AG's office (they enforce IPRA) or the district court. You will need your original request, the agency's response, and the date.

New to IPRA? The New Mexico Foundation for Open Government (NMFOG) is the state's go-to resource — they have a sample request letter, an appeals template, and will answer questions by email.

Attend a City Commission meeting

The City Commission meets at the Donald E. Carroll Commission Chambers, City Hall, 1376 E. 9th Street. Regular meetings are typically the first and third Tuesday of the month at 6:30 PM. Workshop / budget meetings are scheduled separately.

  • Check the city website for the posted agenda and any special meetings.
  • Public comment is usually the first agenda item. Sign up at the door.
  • You don't need to be an expert. A two-minute statement from a resident carries real weight.

Contact your Commissioners

A short, polite email to your Commissioner(s) is one of the most effective things you can do. The current Commission as of May 2026:

  • Mayor Sharon McDonald
  • Mayor Pro-Tem Joshua Rardin
  • Commissioner Baxter Pattillo
  • Commissioner Stephen Burnett
  • Commissioner Warren Robinson
  • Commissioner Al Hernandez
  • Commissioner Mark Tapley

Email addresses and phone numbers are on the city website. A few tips for an effective message:

  • Be specific. Reference a particular document or meeting (e.g. "the May 4, 2026 budget workshop").
  • Ask a question, not just an opinion. "What is the retention period for ALPR data?" gets a more useful response than "ALPRs are bad."
  • Keep it short. A few paragraphs is plenty.
  • Be respectful. Commissioners are more responsive to constituents who treat them like neighbors.

Questions worth asking

Drawing from what is and isn't in the public record so far, here are specific questions residents can raise. These are information requests, not accusations.

  • What is the data retention period for ALPR data, and is it set by the contract or by city policy?
  • Which outside agencies (local, state, federal) have been granted access to Alamogordo's ALPR data, and under what authority?
  • Where is the data stored, and is the City able to fulfill an IPRA request for footage that was not downloaded as evidence?
  • Why is there no separate line item in the budget for the Flock contract?
  • What is the total number of ALPR cameras, and where are they located?
  • What is the cancellation clause, and what is the process for ending the contract?
  • How many cases have used ALPR data, and in what kinds of cases?
